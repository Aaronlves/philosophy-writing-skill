# Philosophy Writing

Make the philosophical job of each passage explicit while adapting the form to its genre.

## Philosophy-First Boundary

Organize prose around philosophical questions, claims, concepts, distinctions, interpretations, reasons, arguments, objections and replies, cases, and dialectical burdens. Do not impose scientific-paper structures such as hypothesis-method-results-discussion, treat literature review as systematic evidence aggregation, or present empirical findings as self-sufficient answers to conceptual, normative, metaphysical, or interpretive questions.

When scientific or empirical material is philosophically relevant, distinguish the reported finding, the bridge premise, and the philosophical conclusion. Preserve the difference between technical scientific verification and assessment of the material's philosophical role.

## Core Commitments

- State the thesis or controlling interpretive claim early; for substantial work, follow it with a roadmap.
- Make the inferential, interpretive, explanatory, or dialectical path visible.
- Preserve the user's intended thesis, distinctions, argumentative burden, and status labels unless the user asks for substantive reconstruction.
- Label alternative arguments and substantive changes rather than silently replacing the user's view.
- Never promote exploratory material into a commitment merely by polishing it.
- Separate source exposition, an opponent's view, charitable reconstruction, criticism, and the author's own claim.
- Verify source support before relying on a source-specific premise. Leave citation-format cleanup until the later revision pass.
- Mark uncertainty, missing support, and unresolved objections directly.

This skill handles drafting, revision, and local review aimed at improving the text while preserving its project role. It does not replace an independent peer review verdict.

## Task Triage

Before writing, identify what the user has supplied:

- genre and task mode;
- audience, length, and stage;
- local thesis or controlling claim;
- larger-project thesis, if any;
- passage or section function;
- argument and definition status;
- source constraints and citation style;
- file-update permission and privacy boundary.

Proceed with reasonable assumptions when details are missing, but state assumptions that materially affect the result. Do not confuse a local section claim with the thesis of the whole work.

Use the narrowest file mode:

- `advisory-only`: answer in chat without file edits;
- `patch-proposal`: provide exact replacement text without writing files;
- `authorized-file-edit`: edit only the files and sections authorized for the current task.

## Drafting and Revision Workflow

1. State the passage's local function and controlling claim in one sentence.
2. Identify what the reader must know and what the passage must establish, interpret, describe, or explain.
3. For substantial work, build a logical or dialectical outline first and a prose or section outline second.
4. Give the reasons, textual grounds, examples, or explanatory considerations needed for the claim.
5. State scope, modality, contrast class, and key definitions where they affect what follows.
6. Present the strongest relevant pressure or alternative reading when the genre and length warrant it.
7. Reply, concede, distinguish, narrow, or leave the issue explicitly unresolved.
8. Explain how the passage advances the larger argument or inquiry.
9. Check source support and philosophical fidelity before treating the draft as reliable.
10. Polish transitions and sentences only after the argumentative or interpretive relations are clear.

For small tasks, apply only the steps that matter. Do not force an outline before a requested one-sentence or one-paragraph revision.

## Genre Sensitivity

- Analytic argument: make thesis, grounds, inferential route, objections, and replies explicit where useful.
- Historical or exegetical writing: distinguish textual claim, interpretive claim, rival reading, textual evidence, and present philosophical significance.
- Phenomenological or descriptive writing: distinguish description, example, conceptual articulation, explanatory claim, and any normative or metaphysical conclusion.
- Formal philosophy: define notation and assumptions, explain the derivation, and state its philosophical interpretation and limits.
- Normative philosophy: identify the normative target, claim type, scope, strength, supporting reasons or values, bridge premises, conflicts, defeat conditions, and the response or judgment favored.
- Case- or thought-experiment-driven philosophy: state the stipulations, imported assumptions, intended verdict, modal role, target principle, nearby variants, and what the case can and cannot establish.
- Empirically informed philosophy: distinguish the scientific claim, its warranted scope, the philosophical bridge, and the resulting conceptual, normative, metaphysical, or explanatory claim; do not turn the passage into a scientific results report.
- Literature review or debate exposition: organize by questions, positions, pressures, and source roles rather than by author catalogue, unless chronology is itself explanatory.
- Thesis, proposal, book, or other long-form work: distinguish the global thesis from local burdens and track how each section contributes to the whole.

Do not force premise-conclusion formalization when it distorts the genre. Always preserve responsibility for what is claimed and how it is supported.

## Review and Repair Priorities

For revision-oriented review, diagnose in this order:

1. thesis or controlling-claim mismatch;
2. missing reason, textual ground, or inferential bridge;
3. scope, modality, equivocation, or concept drift;
4. unsupported or overextended source claim;
5. objection or rival reading not answered;
6. passage or section function unclear;
7. structure or transition that names a relation without establishing it;
8. sentence-level awkwardness.

Identify whether a proposed repair changes the thesis, definition, scope, burden, concession, or source role. If it does, flag it as substantive and offer it separately unless the user requested that change.

## Style

- Prefer plain, precise prose over display of sophistication.
- Keep terminology stable; do not vary words merely for style when the variation may imply a new concept.
- Use signposts only when they name a real relation.
- Use examples when they clarify a distinction, pressure, or scope condition; do not treat an absent example as proof that a claim fails.
- Treat topic sentences, short sentences, and first-person signposting as defeasible aids, not universal rules.
- Calibrate technical vocabulary and explicitness to the audience and genre.
- Use measured evaluative language and do not say a view “shows” more than it establishes.
