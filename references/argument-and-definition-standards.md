# Argument and Definition Standards

## Arguments

For an argument or argumentative passage, make clear:

- conclusion or target claim;
- premises, grounds, or explanatory considerations;
- inferential relation;
- scope, modality, and claimed force;
- hidden assumptions and burden of proof;
- relation to counterexamples or rival explanations;
- strongest relevant objection;
- cost of the best available reply.

Do not impose deductive form on abductive, transcendental, genealogical, analogical, conceptual, or interpretive arguments. Reconstruct the form that best captures the actual support relation.

When repairing an argument, diagnose whether the problem is a false or unsupported premise, invalid or weak inference, ambiguous term, missing bridge, overstrong conclusion, scope shift, modal shift, burden shift, or mismatch between evidence and claim. Label any new premise or charitable repair as the analyst's proposal rather than attributing it to the original author.

Originality and opponent-acceptability are context-sensitive merits, not universal validity conditions. Do not claim novelty without an adequate literature basis, and do not reject an argument merely because an opponent disputes one of its premises; identify the burden that dispute creates.

## Definitions and Distinctions

Identify what kind of formulation is at issue:

- necessary condition;
- sufficient condition;
- biconditional analysis;
- stipulation;
- explication or rational reconstruction;
- ordinary-language characterization;
- source-derived definition;
- operational or working formulation.

Then check:

- scope and contrast class;
- explanatory or argumentative role;
- whether each clause does work;
- central cases, counterexamples, edge cases, and impaired or limiting cases where relevant;
- circularity, regress, overbreadth, underbreadth, and equivocation;
- relation to nearby terms and to source terminology;
- consequences of narrowing, broadening, or changing the formulation.

Do not treat a slogan, example, necessary condition, diagnostic mark, or consequence as a complete definition unless the text warrants that status. Never change a load-bearing definition silently.

## Objections and Replies

An objection should target a specific thesis, premise, inference, definition, interpretation, example, scope condition, method, or explanatory burden.

Mark whether it is internal or immanent, external, source-raised, opponent-attributed, or analyst-generated. Present the strongest relevant version before replying.

A reply may deny a premise, distinguish cases, challenge the inference, accept a cost, narrow the thesis, reinterpret the target, expose a presupposition, or concede a weaker conclusion while preserving the central claim. State what the reply gives up and what pressure remains. Do not answer by merely restating the thesis.

## Thought Experiments and Cases

Identify whether a case functions as a counterexample, possibility argument, intuition elicitor, explanatory model, analogy, illustration, or conceptual diagnostic. State its stipulations, imported background assumptions, modal status, target principle, and intended verdict. Test nearby variants, case-description neutrality, idealizations, representativeness, and whether the elicited judgment presupposes the disputed thesis.

## Normative Claims

Distinguish reasons, obligations, permissions, fittingness, value, virtue, and rational requirements. State the bearer and target, domain, scope, strength, and response or judgment favored. Identify the supporting reasons or values, their grounding, justifying, weighing, enabling, or defeating role, and any bridge from descriptive, psychological, explanatory, phenomenological, or evaluative premises to the normative conclusion.
