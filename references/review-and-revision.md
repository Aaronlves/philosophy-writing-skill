# Review and Revision

## Preservation Boundary

Before revising, state the intended thesis or local claim, the passage function, and any status labels the user supplied. Preserve them unless the user asks for substantive reconstruction.

Flag separately any proposal that changes:

- thesis or conclusion;
- scope, modality, or strength;
- definition or contrast class;
- burden of proof;
- concession or objection-reply relation;
- source attribution or source role;
- exploratory, accepted, rejected, or unresolved status.

## Review Priorities

1. Does the passage's controlling claim match what its reasons or textual grounds establish?
2. Does the passage serve its local and larger function?
3. Are support relations, assumptions, scope, and force explicit enough for the genre?
4. Are central terms stable and distinctions preserved?
5. Are source-specific claims supported at the needed level?
6. Are serious objections or rival readings answered rather than merely named?
7. Does the structure make the intellectual movement visible?
8. Does sentence-level polish preserve the philosophy?

## Revision Passes

1. Diagnose the substantive problem and state the intended function.
2. Verify source support early for any premise or interpretation the revision will rely on.
3. Repair the logical, interpretive, or explanatory structure.
4. Repair section and paragraph order, signposting, and transitions.
5. Tighten scope, terminology, examples, and measured evaluative language.
6. Clean citation form, punctuation, rhythm, and surface style last.
7. Recheck that the revision did not silently change the thesis or status.

## Common Problems

- Literature catalogue: sources are listed but no debate or inquiry is built.
- Rhetorical transition: “therefore” or “however” appears without the corresponding relation.
- Concept drift: a term changes sense, scope, or argumentative role without notice.
- Overclaiming: prose says a source establishes what it only suggests, motivates, reports, or leaves open.
- Decorative objection: an objection appears but does not pressure a determinate target.
- Definition by slogan: a formula is given without scope, role, or counterexample handling.
- Unmarked repair: an analyst's stronger argument is presented as the user's or source's own.
