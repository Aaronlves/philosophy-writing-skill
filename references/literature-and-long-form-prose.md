# Literature Review and Long-Form Philosophy Prose

## Literature Review

Write a literature review as an account of a problem-space and an argument for why a question remains live.

When the material supports it:

1. introduce the problem or organizing question;
2. identify the main positions or strategies;
3. explain what each position clarifies or solves;
4. identify the remaining pressure, disagreement, or transition;
5. locate the present project without treating partial allies as full supporters.

Classify sources by warranted role: target, rival, partial ally, background, precursor, objection source, conceptual resource, case source, terminology source, or methodological model. Distinguish what a source explicitly argues from the local use proposed for it.

Do not organize by isolated author summaries unless the task is an annotated bibliography or the historical sequence itself explains the debate.

## Debate and Question-Focused Exposition

1. Give a compact working characterization of the topic.
2. State the question or pressure that makes it philosophically live.
3. Divide the terrain by questions or problems.
4. Introduce positions as answers or strategies.
5. Explain the attraction and burden of each position before criticizing it.
6. Use cases and distinctions to clarify pressure, not as decoration.
7. Mark scope boundaries and unresolved transitions.
8. End major sections by showing why the next issue arises.

The goal is structured intelligibility, not neutrality without a point of view.

## Source Layers in Prose

Keep separate:

- the source's explicit claim;
- the source's report of another view;
- a checked reconstruction;
- the writer's evaluation;
- the writer's own positive claim.

Verify contested, source-specific claims before using them as premises. Mark unavailable locators or citation checks rather than filling gaps from memory. A citation credits a source; it does not replace the reasons the prose must give.

## Long-Form Section Control

Identify each section's function: orientation, definition, problem motivation, textual interpretation, debate reconstruction, premise defense, objection, reply, limitation, implication, or transition.

Preserve the difference between the whole-work thesis and local claims. Use a logical or dialectical outline to control what must be established and a separate prose outline to control presentation. Draft introductions late when the project's central argument and objections are still unstable.

Transitions should explain why the next section is needed, not merely announce that it comes next.
